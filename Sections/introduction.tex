\section{Introduction}

Vision-language-action (VLA) policies are becoming a promising route toward
general-purpose robot manipulation. By scaling vision-language pretraining and
robot demonstrations, recent robot foundation models can follow language
instructions and transfer across tasks and embodiments~\citep{brohan2022rt1,
brohan2023rt2,team2024octo,kim2024openvla}. Yet spatially reliable control
requires more than recognizing the instructed object: the policy must also know
where its own body is in the image. We refer to this capability as
\emph{visual self-grounding}, the binding between proprioceptive state and
visual evidence of the robot's own arm, gripper, or end-effector.

We motivate visual self-grounding with a two-stage diagnostic. At the behavior
level, we laterally shift the wrist-camera view while keeping the physical
robot-scene state, language instruction, proprioception, and ground-truth
action fixed. A spatially grounded policy should keep the same robot-centric
action, but current VLAs can produce trajectories that shift in the same
direction as the image. This co-directional drift suggests that VLA control can
rely on image-space shortcuts, such as pixel coordinates, camera correlations,
or object-location priors, rather than stable robot-scene geometry.

We then probe the model-level source of this shortcut behavior. In both
simulation and real-robot scenes, robot-centric prompts such as ``Where is the
robot itself?'' often produce attention or token-relevance maps that fail to
localize the visible gripper or end-effector. This suggests that the policy may
identify the instructed object without visually grounding the body that will
execute the action. We therefore attribute the image-space shortcut to missing
visual self-grounding: action supervision can reward correct trajectories under
the training camera without requiring visual tokens to encode where the robot
appears.

Recent spatial and grounding-oriented VLA methods make important progress, but
they do not directly provide a self-supervised signal for visual
self-grounding. Existing approaches improve spatial reasoning through visual
trajectory prompts, 3D representations, camera-space action formulations, view
synthesis, or explicit treatments of proprioception~\citep{zheng2025tracevla,
qu2025spatialvla,zhang2025ocvla,gu2026vistabot,zhao2025statefree,
lu2026gap}. These methods make task space, viewpoint, or state more explicit,
yet they primarily supervise task execution rather than the visual binding
between proprioceptive change and the acting body. What is missing is a
training signal that tells the VLA where its own visible embodiment is,
without manual robot-part masks, external grounding datasets, or stronger
teacher models.

\textsc{ProprioVLA} addresses this gap by turning robot interaction itself
into self-grounding supervision. The key observation is that visual change from
the acting body is synchronized with proprioceptive change. Temporal image
differences and optical flow capture residual visual change, while
proprioceptive changes indicate when that change should correspond to the robot
body rather than unrelated background motion. By aligning these two sources,
\textsc{ProprioVLA} constructs confidence-weighted token-level self-grounding
targets over the visual-token grid using only observations and states already
present in robot demonstrations.

Building on these targets, \textsc{ProprioVLA} trains a lightweight
learned-query grounding head over the VLA visual representation. The head reads
visual tokens through cross-attention and predicts a self-grounding
distribution, which is supervised to recover the state-aligned targets with a
normalized KL objective. Importantly, this branch is auxiliary: the mined
targets are used only during training, the action path never consumes them,
and the policy interface is unchanged at inference. The effect is to regularize
shared visual tokens toward visible embodiment while preserving the standard
action-supervised VLA objective.

We evaluate \textsc{ProprioVLA} in simulation and real-robot manipulation,
including controlled camera-perturbation probes that isolate view-induced
action drift. Across settings, \textsc{ProprioVLA} improves task success and
robustness over action-supervised VLA adaptation.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We identify a missing-self failure mode in VLA policies: camera-only
    viewpoint shifts expose image-space shortcuts in control, and model-level
    visual probes attribute this behavior to missing visual self-grounding of
    the gripper or end-effector.
    \item We introduce State-Aligned Residual Flow (SARF), which turns
    proprioceptively aligned optical-flow and image-difference cues into
    confidence-weighted token-level self-grounding targets without manual
    masks, external grounding datasets, or stronger foundation-model
    supervision.
    \item We propose \textsc{ProprioVLA}, a self-supervised proprioceptive
    alignment approach that trains a learned-query grounding head with
    normalized KL supervision, improving task success and robustness in
    real-robot and simulation environments.
\end{itemize}
