\section{Introduction}

Vision-language-action (VLA) policies are becoming a promising route toward
general-purpose robot manipulation. By scaling visual-language pretraining and
robot demonstrations, recent robot foundation models can follow language
instructions and transfer across tasks and embodiments~\citep{brohan2022rt1,
brohan2023rt2,team2024octo,kim2024openvla}. Yet spatially reliable control
requires a capability that is easy to overlook: the policy must ground the
action not only to the instructed object, but also to the body that executes
the action. We refer to this capability as \emph{self-grounding}, the binding
between proprioceptive state and visual evidence of the robot's own gripper or
end-effector.

We make this missing requirement concrete with two diagnostic findings. First,
when the physical robot-scene state is fixed and only the camera viewpoint is
laterally shifted, VLA-predicted trajectories shift in the same image
direction. Second, even robot-centric prompts such as ``Where is the robot
itself?'' often produce attention maps that fail to localize the gripper or
end-effector. Together, these findings reveal that task grounding does not
imply self-grounding: a policy may identify the instructed object while failing
to anchor its action prediction to the visible body that will execute it.

This failure suggests that the action frame learned by current VLA adaptation
can remain entangled with the image frame. Standard imitation or diffusion
action losses reward correct trajectories under the training observation
distribution, but they do not require the visual representation to encode where
the robot appears. Proprioception may be available as an input, yet it can act
as a separate state cue while visual tokens exploit pixel-coordinate shortcuts,
camera-specific correlations, or object-location priors. Such policies can
therefore appear competent in-distribution while exhibiting co-directional
trajectory drift under camera-only perturbations.

Recent spatial and grounding-oriented VLA methods make important progress, but
they do not directly provide a self-supervised signal for self-grounding.
Existing approaches improve spatial reasoning through visual trajectory
prompts, 3D representations, camera-space action formulations, view synthesis,
or explicit treatments of proprioception~\citep{zheng2025tracevla,
qu2025spatialvla,zhang2025ocvla,gu2026vistabot,zhao2025statefree,
lu2026gap}. These methods make task space, viewpoint, or state more explicit,
yet they primarily supervise task execution rather than the visual binding
between proprioceptive change and the acting body. What is missing is a
training signal that tells the VLA where its own visible embodiment is, without
manual robot-part masks, external grounding datasets, or stronger teacher
models.

\textsc{ProprioVLA} addresses this gap by turning robot interaction itself
into self-grounding supervision. The key observation is that visual change from
the acting body is synchronized with proprioceptive change. Temporal image
differences and optical flow capture residual visual change, while
proprioceptive changes indicate when that change should correspond to the robot
body rather than unrelated background motion. By aligning these two sources,
\textsc{ProprioVLA} constructs
confidence-weighted token-level self-grounding targets over the visual-token
grid using only observations and states already present in robot
demonstrations.

Building on these targets, \textsc{ProprioVLA} trains a lightweight
learned-query grounding head over the VLA visual representation. The head reads
visual tokens through cross-attention and predicts a self-grounding
distribution, which is supervised to recover the state-aligned targets with a
normalized KL objective. Importantly, this branch is auxiliary: the mined
targets are used only during training, the action path never consumes them,
and the policy interface is unchanged at inference. The effect is to regularize
shared visual tokens toward visible embodiment while preserving the standard
action-supervised VLA objective.

We evaluate \textsc{ProprioVLA} in simulation and real-robot manipulation,
including controlled camera-perturbation probes that isolate view-induced
action drift. Across settings, \textsc{ProprioVLA} improves task success and
robustness over action-supervised VLA adaptation. Ablations further show that
state-aligned target mining outperforms raw motion saliency alone, and that
normalized KL provides a stable distribution-matching objective across visual
token counts and target sharpness.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We identify a missing-self failure mode in VLA policies: camera-only
    viewpoint shifts can induce co-directional shifts in predicted robot
    trajectories, while robot-centric prompting can still fail to localize the
    gripper or end-effector.
    \item We introduce State-Aligned Residual Flow (SARF), which turns
    proprioceptively aligned optical-flow and image-difference cues into
    confidence-weighted token-level self-grounding targets without manual
    masks, external grounding datasets, or stronger foundation-model
    supervision.
    \item We propose \textsc{ProprioVLA}, a self-supervised proprioceptive
    alignment approach that trains a learned-query grounding head with
    normalized KL supervision, improving task success and robustness in
    real-robot and simulation environments.
\end{itemize}
