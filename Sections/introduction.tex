\section{Introduction}

Vision-language-action (VLA) policies have emerged as a promising paradigm for
generalist robot manipulation, scaling vision-language pretraining with robot
demonstrations to produce language-conditioned controllers across tasks and
embodiments~\citep{brohan2023rt1roboticstransformerrealworld,brohan2023rt2visionlanguageactionmodelstransfer,octomodelteam2024octoopensourcegeneralistrobot,kim2024openvlaopensourcevisionlanguageactionmodel,black2026pi0visionlanguageactionflowmodel,embodimentcollaboration2025openxembodimentroboticlearning}. Yet, the spatial
representations that support reliable closed-loop control remain insufficiently
understood. Recent work has begun to address this issue by injecting 3D
structure, spatial action representations, point-cloud or depth cues, or
external spatial supervision into VLAs~\citep{zhen20243dvla3dvisionlanguageactiongenerative,qu2025spatialvlaexploringspatialrepresentations,li2025pointvlainjecting3dworld,li2025bridgevlainputoutputalignmentefficient,sun2025geovlaempowering3drepresentations,li2025spatialforcingimplicitspatial}. More broadly, representation supervision has shown
promise for improving visual and spatial representations in multimodal and
generative models~\citep{wang2024reconstructivevisualinstructiontuning,yu2025representationalignmentgenerationtraining,huang20253drsmllmsneed3daware}. However,
these approaches primarily supervise scene- or object-level spatial
understanding, often through external teachers or explicit 3D signals. In
contrast, a more basic requirement for robot control remains underexplored:
whether a VLA visually grounds the robot body that executes the action.

In this work, we identify a systematic failure pattern suggesting that current
VLAs can rely on image-space shortcuts rather than stable robot-scene geometry.
We laterally shift the wrist-camera observation while keeping the physical
robot-scene state, language instruction, proprioceptive state, and ground-truth
action fixed. A spatially grounded policy should preserve the same
robot-centric action under this intervention. Instead, we observe that predicted
trajectories can drift in the same direction as the image shift, indicating
sensitivity to pixel-coordinate correlations or camera-specific object-location
priors. We further probe the visual representation with robot-centric queries
and find that the model often fails to localize the visible gripper or
end-effector, even when it recognizes the task-relevant object. This suggests a
missing capability we call \emph{visual self-grounding}: binding proprioceptive
state to visual evidence of the robot's own embodiment.

To address this gap, we introduce \textsc{ProprioVLA}, a self-supervised
alignment approach to incentivize self-grounding capability in VLA policies.
\textsc{ProprioVLA} guides the policy to localize its own embodiment by
aligning learned grounding queries with state-aligned residual flow targets
mined from robot interaction. The core insight is simple: when only the robot is
actuated, visual change aligned with proprioceptive change reveals the robot
itself. This makes it possible to derive self-grounding supervision directly
from robot demonstrations, without manual masks, external grounding datasets,
or stronger foundation-model supervision.

To operationalize this insight, \textsc{ProprioVLA} first constructs State-Aligned Residual Flow (SARF) targets
from demonstration trajectories. SARF combines optical flow and image
differences with proprioceptive state changes to identify residual visual
motion likely caused by the robot body, and projects this signal onto the VLA
visual-token grid as confidence-weighted self-grounding targets.

Given these targets, \textsc{ProprioVLA} trains a lightweight Proprioceptive
Alignment Module (PAM) to make the VLA visual tokens recover where the acting
body appears. PAM applies learned grounding queries to image-token embeddings
from the VLM's final hidden states and predicts a self-grounding distribution,
which is matched to the SARF target with a normalized KL objective. Because
this branch is auxiliary, SARF flow targets and pseudo-labels are needed only
during training; at inference, the policy uses the original VLA action
interface unchanged.

We evaluate \textsc{ProprioVLA} in both simulation and real-robot
manipulation. Across settings, it improves task success and robustness over
action-supervised VLA adaptation, reduces view-induced trajectory drift under
controlled camera perturbations, and strengthens localization of the visible
gripper or end-effector. These results suggest that visual self-grounding is a
critical representational capability for spatially reliable VLA control.

Our contributions are summarized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We identify a missing-self failure mode in VLA policies: under
    camera-only lateral perturbations, predicted robot trajectories can drift
    with the image shift, revealing image-space shortcuts rather than stable
    robot-centric grounding. We further connect this failure to weak visual
    self-grounding through interpretable representation probes.

    \item We introduce \textsc{ProprioVLA}, a self-supervised proprioceptive
    alignment approach that induces visual self-grounding by aligning learned
    grounding queries with state-aligned residual flow targets mined from robot
    interaction, without requiring manual masks, external grounding datasets,
    or stronger foundation-model supervision.

    \item Experiments in simulation and real-robot manipulation show that
    \textsc{ProprioVLA} improves task success and robustness over
    action-supervised VLA adaptation, while reducing view-induced trajectory
    drift and improving localization of the visible gripper or end-effector.
\end{itemize}
