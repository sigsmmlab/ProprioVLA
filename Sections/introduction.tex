\section{Introduction}

Vision-language-action (VLA) policies aim to map language instructions and
visual observations to robot actions, making them a promising interface for
generalist manipulation. Recent robot foundation models have shown that broad
pretraining and large robot datasets can yield policies that transfer across
tasks and embodiments~\citep{brohan2022rt1,brohan2023rt2,team2024octo,kim2024openvla}.
For manipulation, however, success depends on more than recognizing task
objects in pixels: the policy must predict motions in the robot's coordinate
frame while observing the scene through a camera projection. Spatial
robustness under changes in viewpoint is therefore a core requirement for
reliable VLA control.

Despite this progress, we find that fine-tuned VLA policies can remain weakly
grounded in their own embodiment. In controlled camera-perturbation
evaluations where the underlying robot-scene state is unchanged, lateral
camera shifts induce co-directional shifts in predicted end-effector
trajectories. This failure mode suggests that a policy can exploit image-space
shortcuts, such as correlations between pixel coordinates and actions, rather
than learning a camera-independent relation among the robot state, the
end-effector, and task objects. Such shortcuts are especially problematic
during fine-tuning, where a model may match demonstrations on a fixed visual
distribution while remaining brittle when the same physical state is viewed
from a different perspective.

The key observation behind \textsc{ProprioVLA} is that the end-effector forms
a natural anchor between visual evidence and proprioceptive state. A grounded
policy should not only identify task-relevant objects, but also know where its
own manipulator is and how the manipulator's visual motion corresponds to the
robot's underlying kinematics. We therefore formulate proprioceptive grounding
as a self-supervised fine-tuning problem: the representation used for action
prediction should localize the visible end-effector and preserve enough
information to reconstruct the end-effector state.

We instantiate this idea in \textsc{ProprioVLA}, a self-supervised framework
for proprioceptive grounding in VLA policies. In our implementation, a
lightweight front-view latent adapter initializes a small set of grounding
latents from the current end-effector state and cross-attends them to image
tokens. The adapter predicts both an attention distribution over candidate
end-effector locations and a reconstructed proprioceptive state. Training
augments the original action-prediction objective with two complementary
losses: a confidence-weighted localization loss over pseudo end-effector
targets, and a state reconstruction loss from the grounded latent
representation. The adapter is deliberately kept lightweight and inspectable,
so the original VLA action pathway remains intact while the model is explicitly
encouraged to align visual tokens with robot state.

A practical obstacle is that end-effector locations are rarely annotated in
robot demonstration datasets. Rather than relying on manual labels, external
grounding datasets, or supervision from a stronger foundation model,
\textsc{ProprioVLA} obtains grounding supervision from robot interaction
itself. The pseudo-label generator uses a motion-kinematic consistency
principle: the true end-effector region should be locally salient under visual
motion and temporally consistent with proprioceptive activity. It combines
frame differences, optical flow, and robot-state changes to produce
confidence-weighted front-view targets, then filters them with confidence,
temporal-consistency, bounding-box, and image-boundary checks. Low-confidence
frames abstain from supervision, reducing the risk of training on ambiguous
or noisy targets.

We evaluate \textsc{ProprioVLA} on manipulation benchmarks and controlled
camera-perturbation settings. Our default implementation recipe uses
confidence-filtered pseudo labels together with localization and moderate
state-reconstruction losses, and repeated Bridge/SimplerEnv evaluations show
stable task success across spoon, carrot, and basket manipulation tasks.
Ablations further indicate that the gain is not explained by action-only
fine-tuning, and that combining localization with state reconstruction is more
effective than either auxiliary signal alone. These results suggest that
self-supervised proprioceptive grounding is a practical route toward making
VLA policies more spatially reliable.

Our contributions are threefold:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We identify a systematic embodiment-grounding failure in VLA
    policies: camera shifts can induce co-directional shifts in predicted
    end-effector trajectories even when the underlying robot-scene state is
    unchanged.
    \item We propose \textsc{ProprioVLA}, a self-supervised fine-tuning
    framework that grounds VLA representations through the end-effector using
    visual localization and proprioceptive reconstruction objectives.
    \item We introduce an automatic pseudo-labeling pipeline based on
    motion-kinematic consistency, enabling confidence-weighted end-effector
    supervision without manual annotations, external grounding datasets, or
    stronger teacher models.
\end{itemize}
