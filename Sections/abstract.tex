Vision-language-action (VLA) policies have shown strong promise as generalist
controllers for robot manipulation, yet their action predictions can remain
fragile under changes in visual perspective. We identify a systematic grounding
failure: lateral camera shifts induce co-directional shifts in predicted robot
trajectories, suggesting that current VLAs may exploit image-space shortcuts,
such as pixel-coordinate correlations, rather than grounding actions in
proprioceptive state and robot-object geometry.
To bridge this gap, we introduce \textsc{ProprioVLA}, a self-supervised
learning framework that incentivizes self-grounding in VLA policies.
\textsc{ProprioVLA} guides the policy to visually attend to the robot itself
with learned grounding queries, based on the key insight that
\textit{state-aligned residual flow provides implicit supervision for
self-grounding}.
Specifically, \textsc{ProprioVLA} first mines confidence-weighted
self-grounding heatmaps from robot interaction by selecting residual motion that
is salient in the image and aligned with proprioceptive change. It then trains
the learned-query attention with a confidence-weighted normalized KL objective,
matching the attention distribution to these heatmaps while retaining the
standard action-supervised VLA objective. This yields self-supervised
proprioceptive grounding without manual annotations, external grounding
datasets, or stronger foundation-model supervision.
Extensive experiments on both real-robot and simulation
environments show that \textsc{ProprioVLA} improves task success and robustness
over action-supervised VLA adaptation, suggesting that proprioceptive grounding
is a practical route toward spatially reliable VLA control.
