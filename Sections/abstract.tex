Vision-language-action (VLA) policies have shown strong promise for general-purpose robot manipulation. In this study, we reveal a systematic failure mode of current VLAs: under controlled camera perturbations, shifting the camera left or right causes the predicted end-effector trajectory to drift in the same image-space direction, even when the robot state and scene remain unchanged. This observation suggests that VLA policies can rely on RGB-space shortcuts, such as pixel-coordinate correlations, rather than grounding actions in proprioception and in the robot's spatial relationship with surrounding objects. To address this issue, we propose \textsc{ProprioVLA}, a self-supervised framework for proprioceptive grounding in VLA policies. Our key insight is that the end-effector provides a natural cross-modal anchor: by requiring visual representations to localize the robot body and reconstruct its proprioceptive state, the policy is encouraged to associate what it sees with where it physically is. ProprioVLA injects this body-centric inductive bias through two complementary self-grounding signals: a visual grounding objective that pulls attention toward the robot body in the image, and a proprioceptive reconstruction objective that makes the grounded representation recover the end-effector state. Crucially, this supervision comes almost for free from robot interaction itself: across a trajectory, the end-effector is the controllable scene component whose visual motion is coupled to proprioceptive change. ProprioVLA operationalizes this intrinsic supervisory signal by mining confidence-weighted gripper targets from coupled visual-proprioceptive motion, requiring no manual annotations, external grounding datasets, or teacher models. Experiments on manipulation benchmarks and camera perturbation settings show that ProprioVLA improves task success and robustness over standard VLA fine-tuning, indicating that proprioceptive self-grounding is a practical path toward more reliable VLA control.
