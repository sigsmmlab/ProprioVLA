Vision-language-action (VLA) policies have shown strong promise as generalist
controllers for robot manipulation, yet their action predictions can remain
fragile under changes in visual perspective. We identify a systematic failure pattern that lateral camera shifts induce co-directional shifts in predicted robot
trajectories, suggesting that current VLAs may exploit image-space shortcuts,
such as pixel-coordinate correlations, rather than grounding actions in
proprioceptive state and robot-object geometry.
To bridge this gap, we introduce \textsc{ProprioVLA}, a self-supervised
alignment approach that incentivizes self-grounding capability in VLA policies.
\textsc{ProprioVLA} implicitly encourages the policy to localize its own
embodiment by aligning learned grounding queries with state-aligned residual
flow targets mined from robot interaction.
Specifically, \textsc{ProprioVLA} turns proprioceptively aligned visual change,
captured by optical flow and image difference, into token-level self-grounding
targets. It then trains a lightweight learned-query head over visual tokens in
the VLA representation to recover these targets with normalized KL supervision,
regularizing the visual representation to encode the visible embodiment
without manual masks, external grounding datasets, or stronger foundation-model
supervision.
Extensive experiments on both real-robot and simulation
environments show that \textsc{ProprioVLA} improves task success and robustness
over action-supervised VLA adaptation, suggesting that proprioceptive grounding
is a practical route toward spatially reliable VLA control.
