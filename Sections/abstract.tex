Vision-language-action (VLA) policies have shown strong promise for general-purpose robotic manipulation, yet their real-world deployment remains brittle under seemingly minor camera pose changes. We identify a key failure mode behind this brittleness: rather than grounding actions in the relationship between the robot’s body and surrounding objects, existing VLA policies often rely on image-space shortcuts such as the absolute pixel locations of objects and end-effectors. To address this issue, we propose ProprioVLA, a self-supervised framework for proprioceptive grounding in VLA policies. ProprioVLA encourages a policy to visually localize and reconstruct its own embodiment by introducing auxiliary grounding objectives over the robot end-effector and proprioceptive state. Crucially, our method requires no manual end-effector annotations. Instead, it derives pseudo-grounding labels automatically from robot trajectories using motion dynamics, optical flow, visual residuals, and temporal consistency. These labels supervise lightweight grounding and reconstruction modules that can be integrated into existing VLA fine-tuning pipelines. By forcing the policy to model where the robot is in the visual scene and how its body relates to task-relevant objects, ProprioVLA reduces reliance on brittle RGB shortcuts. Experiments on manipulation benchmarks and camera-shifted deployment settings show that proprioceptive grounding improves policy robustness and task success, suggesting that teaching VLA models to “sense themselves” is a practical path toward more reliable embodied intelligence.
